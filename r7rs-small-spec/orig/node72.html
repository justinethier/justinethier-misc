Booleans

The standard boolean objects for true and false are written as #t and #f.indexfile"t" "sharpfoo" aux indexfile"f" "sharpfoo" aux Alternatively, they can be written #true and #false, respectively. What really matters, though, are the objects that the Scheme conditional expressions (`=13`if, `=13`cond, `=13`and, `=13`or, `=13`when, `=13`unless, `=13`do) treat as trueindexfile"true" "rm" aux or falseindexfile"false" "rm" aux . The phrase ``a true value''indexfile"true" "rm" aux (or sometimes just ``true'') means any object treated as true by the conditional expressions, and the phrase ``a false value''indexfile"false" "rm" aux (or ``false'') means any object treated as false by the conditional expressions.

Of all the Scheme values, only #f counts as false in conditional expressions. All other Scheme values, including #t, count as true.

Note: Unlike some other dialects of Lisp, Scheme distinguishes #f and the empty list indexfile"empty list" "rm" aux from each other and from the symbol indexfile"nil" "tt" aux nil.

Boolean constants evaluate to themselves, so they do not need to be quoted in programs.

The `=13`boolean? predicate returns #t if obj is either #t or #f and returns #f otherwise.
