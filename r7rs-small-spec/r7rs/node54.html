Internal definitions

Definitions can occur at the beginning of a body (that is, the body of a indexfile"lambda" "tt" aux lambda, indexfile"let" "tt" aux let, indexfile"let*" "tt" aux let*, indexfile"letrec" "tt" aux letrec, indexfile"letrec*" "tt" aux letrec*, indexfile"let-values" "tt" aux let-values, indexfile"let*-values" "tt" aux let*-values, indexfile"let-syntax" "tt" aux let-syntax, indexfile"letrec-syntax" "tt" aux letrec-syntax, indexfile"parameterize" "tt" aux parameterize, indexfile"guard" "tt" aux guard, or indexfile"case-lambda" "tt" aux case-lambda). Note that such a body might not be apparent until after expansion of other syntax. Such definitions are known as internal definitionsindexfile"internal definition" "rm" main as opposed to the global definitions described above. The variables defined by internal definitions are local to the body. That is, variable is bound rather than assigned, and the region of the binding is the entire body. For example,

An expanded body containing internal definitions can always be converted into a completely equivalent `=13`letrec* expression. For example, the `=13`let expression in the above example is equivalent to


unspecified error makeother^`=13` 


          gobblecr(let ((x 5))  (letrec* ((foo (lambda (y) (bar x y)))            (bar (lambda (a b) (+ (* a b) a))))    (foo (+ x 3))))

Just as for the equivalent `=13`letrec* expression, it is an error if it is not possible to evaluate each expression of every internal definition in a body without assigning or referring to the value of the corresponding variable or the variable of any of the definitions that follow it in body.

It is an error to define the same identifier more than once in the same body.
